Reverse engineering an E-Bike

E-Bike reverse engineering

Intro

A friend recently got a pretty serious electric mountain bike, running a Shimano Steps system. The bike is fantastic helping to assist pedalling up until 25km/h, beyond the rider must battle with a heavy bike (battery weighs a lot) to increase the speed. This is slightly problematic sometimes since on flat sections a non-ebike rider can manage over 25km/h leaving an e-bike rider struggling to keep up with the extra weight. The US though, has a different max speed for this assist cut out, at 35 km/h instead of 25 km/h. Knowing that this parameter could be changed, I went about reverse engineering the Shimano Steps system to adjust this parameter.

Availible bike tools

Firstly was a look into the official bike tools from Shimano to play with the Steps system. The two main tools are:

  • E-TUBE app in iOS and Android
  • SM-PCE02

The E-TUBE app is free,developed by Shimano and communicates with the bike over a Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) connection. It allows users to update the firmware and change certain parameters of the bike such as display configurations, language seetings and the assist speed on the 3 assist modes: ECO, TRAIL and BOOST. However the app does not allow adjustment of the maximum assist speed.

The second tool from Shimano is a SM-PCE02 of hardware: 'SHIMANO's SM-PCE02 PC Linkage Device for STEPS and DI2 groups is used to check system functions, connections, and errors as well as update firmware and customize functions. The SM-PCE02 connects through a Micro USB port.' This tool costs around £160.

Alternative bike tools

Secondly, there were two 'tuning' apps availible to buy from independent developers which seemed to have reverse engineered the Shimano tools and offer a paid way to change this maximum assist speed along with other modifications. There are:

  • STUNLOCKER - (Android, iOS, Windows - requires SM-PCE02)
  • EPLUS - (iOS, Android, Windows - requires SM-PCE02)

The STunlocker is £1.50 (standard app) + £32 (license for tuning), it seems to be the most popular and well designed for the user. (4.4 * rating on google play store)

The EPLUS on the other hand is free for the standard version but £40 for the tuning license. (2.8 * rating on google play store)

Bluetooth reverse engineering

The first thing I wanted to get an idea of was the Bluetooth communication between the app and the bike. Luckily there are some free tools for this - for Mac OSX Bluetility and for Android nRF Connect. Both these tools allows for scanning of BLE devices revealing their services and include the ability to read and write to services.

The results from first starting with Bluetility were:

Here we can see the Bike is the SCE7000 device, with 5 services each with a number of Characteristics. The SCE7000 device functions both as the display and intermediary between the user and the ETUBE system.

In BLE devices, there are standards for the UUIDs for Services and Characteristics. Here the two standard services are Device Information and Battery, the other 3 are custom requiring more effort to uncover their use. The 3 unknown services are:

  1. 000018FF-5348-494D-414E-4F5F424C4500

  2. 000018FE-1212-EFDE-1523-785FEABCD123

  3. 000018EF-5348-494D-414E-4F5F424C4500

1 and 3 both have in common "5348-494D-414E-4F5F424C4500". A search for each UUID in google brings up 1 in a forum on esp32.
